## Your 5 highest-value answers (bold opener verbatim, then the follow-through)

**1. "Isn't the LLM deciding permissions / risk?"**
> **"No — the class key is computed, not inferred. The model narrates; it never authorises."**
The incident-class key is a deterministic fingerprint — `sha256(service | error_code | target_object_type)` from structured fields. A match counts only on extractor-confirmed *field equality*, never on semantic similarity. The model may *propose* a candidate; the deterministic policy engine plus a named human decide what runs. There is no LLM call in any permission decision.

**2. "Is L3 just autonomy?"**
> **"Nothing here is autonomous — L3 is a Standing Approval, and a named human granted it on screen."**
A class becomes eligible after 3 consecutive verified L2 successes with zero rollbacks; the ops lead clicks *Promote to Standing Approval* — that click is an audit event with their identity — and a one-click *Revoke* sits next to it. Any verification failure or rollback demotes the class to L1. **Approval moved earlier in time — it never left the loop.**

**3. "The 15-second fix — how much of that is the model, and is it a stage trick?"**
> **"Almost none of it is inference — that's the point of a remembered fix."**
On an exact fingerprint match to a graduated record we skip LLM triage entirely: retrieve → gate-check → execute → verify are all deterministic. At most one short summary call, with a canned fallback. **The second time is free.** (The paced ~15s on screen is engineered for the room — real work is ~6-8s; never present 15s as a measured latency.)

**4. "N successes graduates a class — can an attacker graduate one with synthetic tickets?"**
> **"N=3 is a per-class policy value the customer sets, and tickets don't graduate anything — verified executions do."**
Graduation counts only executions that passed post-fix verification against the target system's real state, from an authenticated source, with requester ≠ approver on every L2 run. And the promotion still needs the human *Promote* click.

**5. "Why won't ServiceNow / Conduct just build this?"**
> **"They meter the workflow; we delete it — and incumbents acquire this layer, they don't ship it."**
A product whose success metric is "tickets that never needed a fulfiller" attacks the seat-and-ticket licence base (Jeli $29.7M, Moveworks $2.85B are the comps and an exit path). Conduct makes legacy legible; we make it operable — adjacent layer, not a feature.
